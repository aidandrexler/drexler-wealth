If you could purchase a lifetime annuity that paid you a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted income for as long as you lived, was backed by the full faith and credit of the United States government, provided a survivor benefit for your spouse, and offered a return that was effectively risk-free — how much would you pay for it? Most financial planners would tell you to pay almost anything. The product you are describing is Social Security — and you have already paid for it over your working career.

Wade Pfau, in the Retirement Planning Guidebook, calls Social Security "the best annuity money can buy" — and argues that decisions about when to claim it should be made not just as a breakeven calculation but as a retirement income strategy decision. Specifically: delaying Social Security maximizes the guaranteed income floor precisely when that guaranteed income is most valuable — in the later years of retirement when portfolio volatility matters less but longevity risk matters most.

The mechanics are straightforward. For individuals born after 1960, full retirement age is 67. Claiming at 62 permanently reduces the monthly benefit by approximately 30% compared to claiming at 67. Delaying past full retirement age earns delayed retirement credits of 8% per year up to age 70. The difference between claiming at 62 versus age 70 can be 76% or more in monthly benefit — a difference that compounds with inflation adjustments for the rest of your life and your surviving spouse's life.

The breakeven calculation — the age at which total lifetime benefits from delayed claiming exceed total lifetime benefits from early claiming — is typically in the mid-80s. For married couples, however, the analysis is more complex and generally favors delay even more strongly. The higher earner's benefit becomes the surviving spouse's benefit at the first death. Maximizing the higher earner's benefit by delaying to 70 is the equivalent of purchasing inflation-adjusted survivor insurance at a price no private insurer can match.

There are scenarios where early claiming makes sense: serious health conditions that reduce life expectancy, financial need that cannot be met any other way, or a specific tax or coordination strategy with other retirement income sources. For most healthy married couples with adequate retirement savings, however, delaying Social Security — at minimum to full retirement age, and ideally to 70 for the higher earner — is one of the highest-return, lowest-risk planning decisions available. It is not a breakeven calculation. It is a longevity hedge. And it is free.
