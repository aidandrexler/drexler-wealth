---
title: The Quiet Cost of Uncoordinated Advisors
date: May 27, 2026
description: When the estate attorney, CPA, and financial adviser don't talk to each other, the gaps rarely show up until the worst possible moment.
readTime: 4 min read
---

Most affluent families in Palm Beach County did not fail to hire professionals. They hired three of them — an estate attorney, a CPA, and a financial adviser — and assumed the work would integrate itself. It rarely does. Each professional operates inside a silo defined by their license, their software, and their own definition of "done." The attorney marks the estate plan complete when documents are signed. The adviser marks the account transfer complete when assets arrive in the new title. The CPA marks the return filed when the K-1s reconcile. None of those milestones require anyone to confirm that the trust actually controls the assets it was designed to hold.

The failure modes are predictable once you know where to look. Beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and life insurance policies supersede trust language — which means a carefully drafted pour-over trust can be rendered irrelevant by a single outdated TOD form nobody reviewed after a divorce or a business sale. Unfunded trusts are another quiet catastrophe: the revocable living trust exists on paper, but the deed to the Florida residence, the brokerage account, and the operating company interests were never retitled. At death, those assets pass by beneficiary designation or intestacy, not through the trust architecture the family paid to build. For families with annual exclusion gifting programs, Crummey notice failures can unwind years of transfer tax planning — not because the gifts were wrong, but because the procedural requirements that make them complete were never coordinated between counsel and the trustee.

Florida amplifies these risks rather than reducing them. Relocated families often arrive with out-of-state trusts and LLC structures that were correct in their prior jurisdiction but were never reviewed against Florida homestead law, Florida creditor exemptions, or the practical realities of titling property in a county where real estate values and probate exposure are both elevated. The attorney in the prior state does not call the new Florida CPA. The financial adviser who helped with the brokerage rollover does not call the estate attorney who drafted the trust. The family assumes integration because everyone is competent. Competence without coordination is not a strategy.
