Charitable giving and tax planning intersect in ways that most donors never fully exploit. The standard approach — writing a check to a favorite charity at year end — is the simplest but rarely the most efficient. Three alternative approaches can significantly increase the impact of a given charitable budget while reducing the tax cost.

First: giving appreciated assets rather than cash. When you give a publicly traded stock that has increased in value — say, shares purchased at $20,000 now worth $100,000 — directly to a qualified charity, you receive a charitable deduction for the full $100,000 fair market value and pay no capital gains tax on the $80,000 of embedded gain. If you had sold the stock first and given the cash, you would have paid capital gains tax on $80,000 before making the gift. The charity receives the same $100,000 in either scenario; the cost to you is dramatically lower when giving appreciated stock directly. Hallman and Rosenbloom specifically identify gifts of appreciated property to charity as a primary capital gains avoidance strategy in their tax reduction chapter.

Second: the donor-advised fund. A DAF allows you to make a large charitable contribution in a single year — generating the deduction in the year of contribution — while granting the funds to specific charities over multiple years. This is particularly valuable for bunching charitable deductions: instead of giving $20,000 per year for five years and taking a small deduction each year that may not exceed your standard deduction, you contribute $100,000 to a DAF in year one, take the full deduction in that year, and distribute the funds to charities on your own schedule over the following five years. The assets in the DAF grow tax-free while they await distribution.

Third: the qualified charitable distribution (QCD). For individuals over age 70½ with traditional IRAs, the QCD allows a direct transfer of up to $105,000 (indexed) from the IRA to a qualified charity, satisfying all or part of the required minimum distribution without the distribution counting as taxable income. This is significant: a retiree who is charitably inclined and who has taxable RMDs can direct up to $105,000 of those distributions to charity with zero income tax on the amount distributed. The QCD does not generate a charitable deduction — but it does not generate income either, which has the additional benefit of keeping MAGI low for IRMAA and Social Security taxation purposes.

All three strategies benefit from coordination between the financial adviser who manages the assets, the CPA who models the tax impact, and the estate attorney if the charitable giving is part of a broader legacy strategy.
