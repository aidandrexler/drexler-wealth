One of the most persistent misconceptions in wealth planning for physicians, dentists, attorneys, and other licensed professionals in Florida is the belief that practicing through a Professional Limited Liability Company (PLLC) or Professional Association (PA) provides protection against personal malpractice liability. It does not. Florida Statute §621.07 specifically preserves full personal liability for any licensed professional for their own negligence in the performance of professional services — regardless of what entity form they use to practice.

What the entity does — and this is genuinely valuable — is provide inside liability protection. If a patient sues the practice entity for employment practices, for the negligence of another physician in the practice, or for business-related claims, the entity structure can limit the personal exposure of individual physician-owners who did not personally commit the negligent act. A physician whose employee causes a car accident in a practice vehicle has potential exposure; proper entity structure can limit the reach of that claim to practice assets. A physician who personally commits a surgical error has full personal liability regardless of entity form.

The practical implication: for physicians and other licensed professionals, professional liability insurance is not optional and is not supplemental. It is the primary protection. The entity is a layer of organization and business liability management. The insurance is the creditor defense. Gassman and Markham address this directly in their analysis of Florida professional LLC structures — the entity has genuine value, but the professional liability of the individual practitioner is not among the risks it addresses.

For physicians approaching the structuring of a practice, the priority order is: adequate professional liability limits with appropriate tail coverage, umbrella policy sized to personal net worth, then entity structure for business liability management. Any adviser who leads with entity structure without confirming insurance adequacy has the priorities reversed. The malpractice exposure of a Florida physician without adequate insurance is not meaningfully different whether they practice as a sole proprietor, a PA, or a PLLC. The coverage is what matters.
