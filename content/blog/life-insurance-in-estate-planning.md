Life insurance is the financial product that most people buy for reasons that feel personal — protecting a family from the financial impact of a premature death — and most planners present for reasons that feel actuarial. For high-net-worth families, the planning utility of life insurance extends well beyond income replacement, and the analysis is fundamentally mathematical rather than emotional.

Rojeck opens his life insurance chapter with characteristic directness: "Life insurance is not a spiritual concept that simply requires belief. It can be analyzed mathematically." The question is not whether to believe in life insurance. It is whether, in your specific situation, the economics justify the premium. For many HNW families, the answer is yes — but for different reasons than the ones that motivated the initial purchase.

Estate liquidity is the first. A taxable estate is not necessarily a liquid estate. A family whose wealth consists primarily of a closely held business, real estate, and retirement accounts may have significant net worth and very little cash to pay an estate tax bill that is due nine months after the date of death. Without liquidity, assets may have to be sold at distressed prices — or at the wrong time — to meet the obligation. A life insurance policy owned by an ILIT provides a liquid, estate-tax-free pool of capital available precisely when estate liquidity is needed.

Equalization among heirs is the second. A business owner who intends to leave the family business to one child and equivalent wealth to other children faces a practical problem: the business may represent most of the estate's value, and non-business assets may be insufficient to equalize the inheritance. Life insurance can create wealth designated for the non-business heirs, funded during the owner's lifetime with after-tax dollars that are far more efficient than other equalization mechanisms.

Buy-sell funding is the third — discussed separately in the context of the Connelly decision, but worth noting here that the economics of using life insurance to fund a buy-sell obligation are generally favorable. The business or its owners pay premiums to create a pool of capital that will be available when a triggering event occurs. The alternative — relying on accumulated earnings or installment payments from a surviving partner — introduces uncertainty and potential business disruption at exactly the wrong moment.

Hallman and Rosenbloom address life insurance in estate planning in their chapter on basic insurance principles, framing it through the large-loss principle: insurance should be sized to risks that could substantially deplete net worth. For estates with significant tax exposure or business succession complexity, the risk of inadequate estate liquidity qualifies.
