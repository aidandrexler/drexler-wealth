description: S-corporation owners can reduce payroll taxes by taking a portion of their income as distributions rather than salary. But the IRS requires that shareholder-employees receive reasonable compensation for services. Underpaying has audit consequences. Overpaying wastes the benefit.

One of the most commonly discussed tax strategies for business owners is the S-corporation salary/distribution split. The logic is straightforward: W-2 wages paid to a shareholder-employee are subject to payroll taxes (Social Security and Medicare at a combined 15.3% on wages up to the Social Security wage base, and 2.9% above it). S-corporation distributions are not subject to these payroll taxes. A business owner who can shift income from wages to distributions reduces the payroll tax burden on the shifted amount.

The IRS is aware of this incentive and actively audits S-corporations where shareholder-employee wages appear unreasonably low. The requirement is explicit: a shareholder-employee must receive reasonable compensation for services performed. What is reasonable compensation is determined by what similarly qualified employees would be paid for similar services in similar markets. The IRS has successfully challenged S-corporations that paid $0 or minimal wages to active shareholder-employees and attributed all income to distributions.

The less-discussed side of the equation is equally important: overpaying W-2 compensation costs money unnecessarily. Every dollar of W-2 wages above what is needed for other planning purposes — retirement plan contribution capacity, disability insurance benefit calculations, QBI deduction optimization — is a dollar subject to payroll tax that could have been a distribution. The optimization is not simply "minimize salary to minimize payroll taxes." It is "pay reasonable compensation for services, then optimize the salary level within that range for other planning objectives."

Rojeck notes in his business succession chapter that the compensation structure of the business owner — W-2 salary versus distributions — directly affects the financial planning analysis, including retirement plan contribution capacity and the buy-sell funding calculation. Evensky's life balance sheet framework treats the business owner's compensation structure as a key input to the overall wealth management plan, not a separate CPA-only matter. The correct answer for any specific business owner requires the CPA, the financial adviser, and sometimes the business attorney to be looking at the same picture simultaneously.
