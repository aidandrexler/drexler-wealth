On June 6, 2024, the United States Supreme Court decided Connelly v. United States, 144 S. Ct. 1406, unanimously. The decision addressed a question that had divided lower courts for years: when a corporation owns a life insurance policy on a shareholder and uses the proceeds to redeem that shareholder's stock at death, should the insurance proceeds be included in calculating the corporation's fair market value for estate tax purposes?

The Supreme Court said yes. The insurance proceeds are an asset of the corporation on the date of death. They are not offset by the redemption obligation. The corporation's date-of-death value — the value that determines the estate tax on the deceased shareholder's stock — includes the full amount of the life insurance proceeds.

The practical consequence: a business with an operating value of $3 million that holds $2 million in life insurance on a 50% shareholder now has a date-of-death value of $5 million per the Court's holding. The deceased shareholder's 50% interest is valued at $2.5 million. Without the Connelly rule, it would have been valued at $1.5 million. The difference in estate tax exposure at the 40% federal rate is $400,000.

Most entity-purchase buy-sell agreements — where the company owns the policies and redeems the deceased shareholder's interest — were drafted before Connelly and assumed the offset. Many have not been reviewed since the decision. Rojeck describes the buy-sell agreement as "one of the most basic business documents, yet one often absent or poorly drafted" — and notes that the triggering events, the valuation method, and the funding mechanism all require regular review. Post-Connelly, that review is not optional.

Cross-purchase agreements — where each owner owns a policy on the other owners personally, not through the entity — are generally not affected by Connelly. They come with their own administrative complexity, particularly in multi-owner businesses where the number of policies required grows with each additional owner. The post-Connelly response for many businesses will involve restructuring entity-purchase agreements into cross-purchase structures, using insurance LLCs, or other approaches. None of these restructuring options should be executed without coordination between a business attorney, a CPA, and a financial adviser who understands the insurance funding mechanics.

If your buy-sell agreement has not been reviewed by both counsel and your financial adviser since June 2024, it has not been reviewed in light of the most significant development in buy-sell law in a generation.
