---
title: The Florida Homestead Trap
date: May 20, 2026
description: Florida's homestead protection is the strongest in the country. It also disappears the moment property is titled incorrectly.
readTime: 4 min read
---

Florida's homestead protection under Article X, Section 4 of the Florida Constitution is among the most powerful asset protection devices in American law. For many Palm Beach County families, the primary residence is both the largest balance sheet item and the most legally significant one — protected from most forced sale by judgment creditors, subject to special rules in descent and devise, and eligible for the Save Our Homes assessment cap that limits annual property tax increases. These protections are not automatic features of owning a nice house in Florida. They attach to a specific legal status: property used as the owner's primary residence, titled in a manner consistent with constitutional homestead requirements.

The trap appears when families — often at the recommendation of a well-intentioned adviser from another state — deed the homestead into a limited liability company. The logic sounds reasonable: separate the residence from operating business risk, consolidate assets inside an entity, simplify management for a family with multiple properties. In Florida, the logic fails. Transferring homestead into an LLC typically destroys constitutional homestead status because the property is no longer owned by a natural person as their residence. The creditor protection evaporates. The Save Our Homes cap can be reset, producing a sudden and permanent increase in property tax liability. And the restrictions on devising homestead property — which limit how you can leave the residence to heirs if you have a spouse or minor child — may still apply in ways that conflict with how the LLC operating agreement or the out-of-state trust attempts to distribute the asset.

The out-of-state trust issue compounds this for Palm Beach County transplants. Families moving from high-tax jurisdictions frequently arrive with revocable trusts drafted in New York, New Jersey, or Connecticut that assume homestead works the way it works elsewhere. Florida counsel is often engaged years later — if at all — to review whether the trust's funding provisions, the deed's vesting language, and the family's actual use of the property still satisfy Florida's homestead requirements. By then, the LLC may already exist, the cap may already be lost, and unwinding the structure may require a sale, a retitling, and a conversation nobody wanted to have.

Before you deed your Palm Beach County residence into any entity — or accept a template from an adviser who does not practice Florida real property law — ask your attorney one question: *Will this transfer cause my family to lose constitutional homestead protection under Florida Constitution Article X, Section 4, and if so, what is the quantified cost of that loss in both creditor exposure and property tax?*
