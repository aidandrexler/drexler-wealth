When a family relocates from New York, New Jersey, or Illinois to Palm Beach County, they typically have two immediate motivations: the weather and the taxes. Florida has no personal income tax, no state estate tax, and no state tax on retirement income including Social Security, pensions, and IRA distributions. For a high-income family previously paying 10-13% in state income tax, the financial impact of relocation is immediate and significant.

But most relocating families capture only a portion of the available benefit. The reasons are predictable. Their CPA is still in New Jersey, filing returns under New Jersey's framework and thinking in New Jersey terms. Their estate attorney is still in Manhattan, drafting documents that comply with New York law and do not reflect Florida's distinct homestead rules, tenancy-by-entireties framework, or Trust Code. Their financial adviser knows they moved but has not triggered a comprehensive review of their planning documents under Florida law.

Here is what a complete Florida-ization of a family's planning actually involves. First: establishing domicile properly. Florida requires more than just buying a house. Domicile requires physical presence, a Florida driver's license, Florida voter registration, updating your declaration of domicile at the county clerk's office, and changing your banking relationships and professional affiliations to Florida addresses. Incomplete domicile documentation is the primary reason prior-state revenue departments successfully challenge Florida residency claims. Rojeck notes that generally five factors are considered in determining domicile change: physical presence, home, family and business activities, personal property, and documentary evidence.

Second: reviewing and updating estate documents. A New York revocable trust may not comply with Florida Statute §732.4015, which restricts how you can devise your homestead to your heirs. A will that says "I leave my residence to my children equally" may conflict with Florida's constitutional homestead restrictions on devise — and the conflict is not resolved in favor of the will.

Third: leveraging Florida's creditor exemptions. The unlimited homestead exemption under Article X §4 of the Florida Constitution. The tenancy-by-entireties protection for joint spousal accounts under Beal Bank, SSB v. Almand & Associates (Fla. 2001). The unlimited retirement account protection under Fla. Stat. §222.21. The life insurance cash value and annuity proceeds exemption under §222.14. These protections exist automatically — but only if assets are correctly titled and the planning structure reflects Florida, not the prior state.

Relocation is not just a tax event. It is an estate and asset protection planning event. The families who maximize the benefit are the ones who trigger a comprehensive Florida review within the first 12 months of establishing residence. The families who do not often discover the gaps years later, when the cost of fixing them is much higher.
