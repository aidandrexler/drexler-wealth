There is a moment that estate planning attorneys dread. A family arrives after the death of a parent, trust document in hand, expecting a smooth and private administration. The attorney asks to see the brokerage statements. The accounts are titled in the decedent's individual name. So is the house. So are the bank accounts. The trust — carefully drafted, properly signed, witnessed, and notarized — owns nothing. Everything goes through Florida probate, which is a public court process. The inventory of the estate, including every asset the family owns and who inherits what, becomes a public record anyone can search.

This is not a rare scenario. It is the single most common failure in estate planning, and it has a name: the unfunded trust. Florida Statute §736.0401 defines how a trust is created, but creation is only the first step. Funding — the process of actually re-titling assets to the trustee of the trust — is the step that almost every client is responsible for completing and almost no client actually completes without direct, persistent guidance.

What funding means in practice: your brokerage account must be re-titled from "John Smith" to "John Smith, Trustee of the John Smith Revocable Trust dated January 1, 2024." Your real estate must be re-deeded. Your business interests must be assigned. Bank accounts must be re-titled or designated with a payable-on-death designation to the trust. For each asset, the process is different. For each financial institution, the paperwork requirements are different. This is not legally complex — but it requires follow-through that most clients do not prioritize.

The planning implication is straightforward. If you have a revocable trust, audit it. Pull the account statements for every brokerage account, every bank account, and every real estate holding you own. If the name on the account is your individual name — not your trustee name — the trust does not control that asset. Schedule a call with your estate attorney and your financial adviser in the same week. One to update the titling instructions; the other to re-register the accounts. It is a two-hour project that your family will be grateful for.
