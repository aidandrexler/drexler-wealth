title: The Inherited IRA Trap Most Families Don't See Coming
date: June 3, 2026
description: SECURE 2.0 eliminated the lifetime stretch for most non-spouse IRA beneficiaries. The 10-year rule means a large retirement account left to an adult child in their peak earning years could be taxed at the highest federal bracket — and most families don't know it until it is too late.
readTime: 5 min read
---

For many years, inherited IRA planning relied on a simple and powerful concept: a non-spouse beneficiary could stretch required distributions over life expectancy, often over multiple decades. That approach allowed smaller annual distributions, lower annual tax pressure, and continued tax-deferred growth on the undistributed balance. The SECURE Act changed that baseline for most families. Under current law, most non-spouse beneficiaries must fully distribute inherited retirement accounts by the end of year ten after death. As Natalie Choate's 2025 outline explains, there is an additional technical layer: if the original owner died after the required beginning date, annual required minimum distributions generally apply in years one through nine, with full depletion by year ten under IRC §401(a)(9)(H). In practical terms, many heirs now face both timing pressure and tax-rate pressure, especially when the inherited account is large.

That legal shift creates a planning problem that is easy to miss until it is expensive. Imagine an adult child in peak earning years inheriting a substantial traditional IRA. Even if distributions are spread over ten years, the inherited IRA income stacks on top of already high compensation and can push more dollars into top marginal rates. The exact bill depends on income, filing status, deductions, and year-by-year distribution choices, but the pattern is consistent: the compressed distribution window can convert what used to be a long, manageable tax stream into a concentrated high-rate tax event. Florida residents avoid state income tax, which helps, but federal ordinary-income treatment still governs traditional IRA distributions. The family did not do anything wrong; the rules changed. The risk is failing to update beneficiary and tax strategy after the law changed.

There are still meaningful exceptions and distinctions. Surviving spouses generally have the most flexibility and may roll inherited IRA assets into their own IRA. Certain "eligible designated beneficiaries," including some disabled or chronically ill beneficiaries, may still use life-expectancy-based payouts under specific rules. Minor children of the account owner have their own timing framework, with the ten-year clock typically starting at majority. Trust beneficiaries add another layer, because trust drafting that was effective before SECURE may no longer produce intended tax outcomes now. Choate's work emphasizes that trust language, conduit versus accumulation structure, and beneficiary class definitions can all materially change results. The key point for families is that inherited IRA planning is now less automatic and more technical than it appeared under pre-SECURE assumptions.

The action step is immediate and concrete: review every retirement account beneficiary designation with your estate planning attorney and CPA together, then align tax planning with those legal choices. For many households, partial Roth conversions in lower-income years, especially between retirement and required minimum distribution age, may reduce future inherited-account tax drag by shifting dollars into accounts that still face a ten-year distribution window but generally avoid income tax on qualified payouts. As Pfau discusses in *Retirement Planning Guidebook* (2021), distribution timing and tax coordination are central retirement planning decisions, not administrative details.
