---
title: The Retirement Risk Nobody Talks About Enough
date: June 10, 2026
description: Sequence-of-return risk — the danger of a market downturn in the early years of retirement — can derail an otherwise sound retirement plan far more than average returns would suggest. Here is how it works and what to do about it.
readTime: 5 min read
---

During your working years, when you are adding money to retirement accounts instead of pulling money out, the order of market returns usually does not determine success or failure. If you earn strong returns one year and weak returns the next, regular contributions and long time horizons often smooth out the path. But retirement changes the math the day withdrawals begin. If the market drops and you still need to fund living expenses, you must sell more shares at lower prices. Those shares are permanently gone, so they cannot participate in the rebound. Two retirees can hold the same portfolio, earn the same long-run average return, and spend the same amount each year, yet end up with very different outcomes based only on when bad returns arrive. This is sequence-of-return risk. As Wade Pfau explains in the *Retirement Planning Guidebook* (2021), and as the historical SAFEMAX work highlighted, the worst retirement outcomes came from the worst early sequences, not from low average returns across a full lifetime.

The practical takeaway is straightforward and unsettling: retirement success is path-dependent, not just average-return-dependent. William Bengen's 1994 research, which became the foundation for the 4% rule conversation, reviewed rolling 30-year retirement periods and identified retirees starting in 1966 as the most stressed case. They faced a difficult first decade with inflation pressure and weak real returns, and that early damage echoed for decades. The lesson is not that every retiree should use one fixed withdrawal rate forever. The lesson is that the first 5 to 10 years carry disproportionate importance because spending withdrawals amplify market losses when portfolios are most vulnerable. In plain English, the danger is not simply "bad markets." The danger is bad markets at the exact moment your paycheck has stopped and your portfolio has become your paycheck.

Three risk-management moves can materially improve durability. First, keep a true spending reserve, often one to two years of planned withdrawals in cash or short-duration high-quality bonds, so you can spend from reserves instead of selling equities into a decline. That reserve is not "idle money"; it is a shock absorber that buys recovery time. Second, coordinate claiming strategy so at least one spouse, usually the higher earner, delays Social Security when feasible. Pfau repeatedly emphasizes that Social Security is unusually valuable because it is inflation-adjusted, lifetime income that reduces withdrawal pressure exactly when market stress can do the most harm. Third, evaluate whether partial annuitization for essential expenses fits your household. Converting part of the portfolio into guaranteed income can separate baseline spending from market volatility, so your remaining invested assets can recover without forced sales.

The deeper planning point is that retirement resilience depends at least as much on distribution design as on portfolio size. A larger account with an unmanaged withdrawal process can fail sooner than a smaller account with disciplined guardrails, reserve management, and coordinated income timing. This is where integrated planning adds value: stress-testing withdrawals, sequencing income sources, and adjusting spending rules before market stress forces decisions. Sequence risk is not theoretical; it is the core reason retirement plans that look fine in spreadsheets can break in real life. A prudent drawdown framework turns uncertainty into a managed process rather than a guessing game.
