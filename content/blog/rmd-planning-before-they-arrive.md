Required minimum distributions — the mandatory annual withdrawals the IRS requires from traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, and most other tax-deferred retirement accounts — begin at age 73 for individuals born between 1951 and 1958, and at age 75 for those born after 1959 (per SECURE 2.0, enacted 2022). The amounts are calculated by dividing the prior year-end account balance by a life expectancy factor from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table. They are taxed as ordinary income in the year of distribution. They cannot be avoided once they begin — only managed.

The planning challenge is that by age 73, most of the strategic options for managing RMD exposure have significantly narrowed. The Roth conversion window — the years between retirement and the start of RMDs when income is low enough to make conversions tax-efficient — has largely closed. The opportunity to contribute to an employer plan and defer the RMD from that plan while still working has ended. The ability to make qualified charitable distributions (QCDs) from an IRA to reduce the taxable amount of an RMD is available beginning at age 70½ — but only if the infrastructure for charitable giving is in place.

What the decade before age 73 allows: annual Roth conversions timed to the lowest-income years after retirement, converting traditional IRA balances to Roth at rates that may be 22% or 24% federal rather than the 32% or 37% that will apply when Social Security, pension income, and RMDs all stack on top of each other. Charitable planning — specifically, establishing a donor-advised fund or qualifying an existing charitable relationship for QCD treatment — that reduces the taxable portion of RMDs when they begin. Beneficiary designation review that ensures the right people are named as beneficiaries, because the 10-year rule for most non-spouse beneficiaries under SECURE 2.0 makes the identity of each beneficiary a meaningful tax planning decision. And asset location decisions — which assets to hold in which accounts — that maximize the after-tax efficiency of the mandatory distributions when they arrive.

Choate's Estate Planning for Retirement Benefits (Sept. 2025 outline) is the definitive source on the mechanics of RMD rules, applicable ages, and the interaction between the 10-year rule and the required beginning date. The planning implication is clear: the conversation about RMDs should begin no later than age 60 — ideally earlier. The clients who arrive at 73 with a large traditional IRA and no prior planning have run out of time for most of the best options. The clients who began the conversation at 62 have had a decade of optionality.
