There is a particular type of wealth planning problem that sits at the intersection of tax complexity and emotional attachment: the concentrated stock position. An executive who has accumulated $3M in a single employer's stock over twenty years through option grants and restricted share units. A founder whose company went public and whose net worth is now 90% in that company's stock. A family that inherited a position from a grandparent who worked at the company for forty years and never sold a share. In each case, the concentration is both the source of the wealth and the source of the planning problem.

Hallman and Rosenbloom describe the capital gains lock-in problem directly: investors with large paper gains feel effectively trapped because selling means paying a substantial tax on gains accumulated over many years. Diversification — which every portfolio management principle recommends — comes at a cost that can feel enormous when quantified. For a $3M position with a basis of $200,000, the capital gains tax on a full sale at the current long-term rate can exceed $600,000 at the federal level, plus any state tax. That is real money to leave on the table in the name of diversification.

But the alternative — holding the concentrated position indefinitely — carries its own risks that are often underweighted in this analysis. Single-stock risk is not compensated in an efficient market: holding a concentrated position does not increase expected return relative to a diversified portfolio of the same expected risk level; it only increases the variance of outcomes. The stories of concentrated positions going to zero are less prominent than the success stories — but they exist in every industry, and the consequences for an uninsured portfolio are severe.

The practical planning approaches for concentrated positions include: systematic diversification over time timed to manage annual capital gains recognition, exchange funds that allow a position to be contributed to a partnership with other concentrated holders for diversification without triggering gain, charitable gifting of appreciated shares to eliminate embedded gain while generating a deduction, and estate planning strategies that leverage the step-up in basis at death for positions that are unlikely to need to be sold during the owner's lifetime. Each approach has trade-offs. The right answer depends on the size of the position, the basis, the owner's other assets, their income, their tax situation, and their timeline. It requires a financial adviser, a CPA, and often an estate attorney working together.
