There is an asset protection tool available to every married couple in Florida that requires no attorney, no LLC, no trust, and no annual maintenance. It costs nothing to implement and provides immediate protection from individual creditors of either spouse. Most married couples in Florida do not have it — because nobody told them to ask for it.

The tool is tenancy by the entireties, and it applies to bank accounts and brokerage accounts jointly owned by married spouses. When an account is properly titled as tenancy by the entireties, a judgment creditor who has a claim against only one spouse cannot reach that account. The protection applies to individual creditors of either spouse. It does not protect against joint debts that both spouses owe together — a jointly signed mortgage, for example, or a jointly guaranteed business loan.

The Florida Supreme Court confirmed this protection in Beal Bank, SSB v. Almand & Associates (2001), and the court extended and clarified it further in Loumpos v. Bank One (Fla. Dec. 2025). The protection is not automatic for all joint accounts. Many accounts opened at brokerage firms default to "joint tenants with right of survivorship" — which provides survivorship rights but not the same creditor protection. The account agreement must specifically use tenancy by the entireties language, or the Beal Bank presumption may not apply.

The practical step is simple: contact your bank and each brokerage firm where you hold joint accounts with your spouse. Ask whether the account is titled as tenancy by the entireties. If it is not, request the paperwork to change the titling. This is a one-time administrative task. For a married professional couple with significant joint savings, it may be the single highest-return per-hour planning action available to them. Gassman and Markham, in their treatise on Florida and Federal Asset Protection Law (2025 ed.), address tenancy by the entireties in Chapter 2 as one of the foundational pillars of the Florida asset protection framework — available to all married Florida residents and systematically underutilized.

For physicians, attorneys, and other professionals with meaningful liability exposure, TBE titling of joint accounts is not optional. It is the second line of defense after adequate insurance, and it costs nothing to implement.
