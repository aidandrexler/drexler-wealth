A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust — universally known as a GRAT — is one of the most widely used estate tax reduction strategies available to high-net-worth families, and one of the least understood by the families who could benefit from it. The basic structure is elegant: the grantor transfers assets to an irrevocable trust, retains the right to receive an annuity payment from the trust for a fixed term of years, and at the end of the term, whatever remains in the trust passes to the beneficiaries — typically children or other heirs — free of additional gift or estate tax.

The gift tax on the initial transfer is calculated as the present value of the remainder interest using the IRS Section 7520 interest rate as the discount rate. By structuring the annuity payments so that the present value of the retained annuity equals the full value of the assets transferred, the gift tax value of the remainder interest can be reduced to zero or near zero. This is called a "zeroed-out GRAT." If the assets in the trust grow faster than the 7520 rate over the term, the excess appreciation passes to the heirs tax-free. If the assets do not outperform the hurdle rate, the trust terminates with no transfer to heirs — and the grantor has lost nothing except the time value of the planning.

Rojeck illustrates the mechanics with a concrete example in his estate planning chapter: assets transferred at their current value to a GRAT, with a zeroed-out annuity structure, effectively allow all appreciation above the 7520 rate to leave the estate without using any of the grantor's lifetime gift tax exemption. For assets with strong appreciation prospects — business interests, concentrated stock positions, real estate in growth markets — this structure can shift significant wealth to the next generation at minimal tax cost.

Two practical limitations are worth noting. First, GRATs require the grantor to survive the trust term. If the grantor dies during the term, the assets are included back in the estate. Short-term GRATs (2-3 years) reduce this mortality risk. Second, GRATs are most effective in low interest rate environments, when the 7520 hurdle rate is low and easy for invested assets to exceed. In higher rate environments, the bar for outperformance is higher. Neither limitation eliminates the strategy — they inform its design.

GRATs should be designed and executed by an estate planning attorney working in coordination with the financial adviser managing the assets in the trust and the CPA modeling the income tax implications.
