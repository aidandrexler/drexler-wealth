Married couples filing jointly enjoy a significant advantage in the federal income tax system. The tax brackets for married filing jointly are roughly twice as wide as those for single filers, meaning a married couple can earn significantly more before reaching the higher brackets. This advantage is invisible during a marriage. It becomes painfully visible when the first spouse dies.

In the year following a spouse's death, the surviving spouse must file as single — unless they have a qualifying dependent. The same taxable income that was comfortable within a lower bracket under joint filing may now be pushed into the 32% or 35% bracket under single filing. Required minimum distributions from retirement accounts, Social Security benefits, investment income, and any remaining earned income all stack against the narrower single-filer brackets. Pfau calls this the widow's tax penalty — and it is one of the most commonly overlooked planning risks in retirement.

The planning implication is time-sensitive. The window to address the widow's penalty is before the first death — not after. The primary tool is Roth conversion during the joint filing years. Converting traditional IRA assets to Roth while both spouses are alive and while the joint filing brackets are wide reduces the balance of pre-tax retirement accounts that will be subject to required minimum distributions after the first death. A surviving spouse with a large traditional IRA, a Social Security benefit, and required minimum distributions on top of that income can easily find themselves in a bracket that was unimaginable during the joint filing years.

The planning also affects which accounts to draw from first in retirement, how to time Roth conversions across multiple years, and how to think about the order of asset liquidation at death. An adviser who is only modeling portfolio performance without modeling the tax impact on the surviving spouse has modeled less than half the retirement plan. This coordination — between the financial adviser who sees the portfolio and the CPA who sees the tax return — is exactly the kind of integration that most advisory teams do not perform automatically. It requires a specific request. It is worth making.
