Most high-income professionals have life insurance. Far fewer have adequate disability income insurance — and many existing policies will not perform as expected when needed. The reason is the definition of disability in the contract. There are three standard definitions used in disability income contracts: the any occupation definition (you are disabled only if you cannot perform any gainful employment), the own occupation definition (you are disabled if you cannot perform the specific duties of your current occupation), and a split definition (own-occupation for the first two years, then any-occupation thereafter). The difference between these definitions can mean the difference between receiving and not receiving benefits after a disabling event. Hallman and Rosenbloom, in *Private Wealth Management* (9th ed. 2014), state that coverage should be purchased for both accident and sickness, and that the definition of disability is important in determining when a person is considered disabled for purposes of collecting benefits.

For physicians, attorneys, surgeons, and other licensed professionals with specialized skills, the own-occupation definition is the only acceptable standard. A surgeon who loses the use of her dominant hand is disabled from practicing surgery — regardless of whether she could theoretically work as a medical administrator at a fraction of her prior income. An any-occupation policy would deny benefits in this scenario. An own-occupation policy would pay. For a thirty-five-year-old surgeon earning $600,000 per year with thirty working years ahead of them, the present value of their future earnings — what Evensky and Horan call human capital — exceeds $10 million. Disability income insurance protects that human capital. Most high-income professionals are dramatically underinsured against its loss relative to what they spend insuring their financial assets.

Three features beyond the definition deserve attention. Elimination period: how long you wait before benefits begin. Longer waits reduce premiums if you have reserves. Benefit period: how long benefits are paid — to age sixty-five or for life are the standards for high-quality policies. Residual benefits: partial payments if you return to work at reduced income. A surgeon practicing three days per week after a disability should not receive zero benefits under an all-or-nothing definition.

Group disability coverage through an employer or professional association is almost always insufficient for high-income professionals. It typically covers sixty percent of base salary up to a monthly cap, excludes bonuses and partnership distributions, is taxable when employer-paid, and terminates when you leave the employer. Individual own-occupation disability income policies — purchased directly from a carrier and owned by you — are portable, often non-cancellable, and can be structured to cover your actual income including variable compensation. For physicians, the six most competitive carriers for own-occupation individual DI are Guardian, Principal, The Standard, MassMutual, Ameritas, and Ohio National. Getting quotes from all six and comparing definitions carefully is the right process.
