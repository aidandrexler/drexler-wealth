Most people understand that Medicare has a standard premium. Fewer people know that Medicare premiums are means-tested — meaning higher-income retirees pay more, sometimes significantly more, based on their income from two years prior. The Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount, known as IRMAA, adds surcharges to Medicare Part B (medical) and Part D (prescription drug) premiums that can add thousands of dollars per year for high-income households.

The two-year lookback is the critical planning detail. Medicare uses your modified adjusted gross income from two years prior to determine your current year premium tier. A Roth conversion executed in 2024 increases your 2024 MAGI — which determines your 2026 Medicare premiums. A business sale that closes in 2025 affects your 2027 Medicare costs. Most financial plans that model Roth conversions or business exit scenarios do not automatically incorporate the IRMAA impact — because it requires the financial adviser and the CPA to be working from the same multi-year income projection simultaneously.

Pfau specifically identifies IRMAA as one of the three reasons a CPA must be in the room before a Roth conversion is executed — alongside the Social Security provisional income rules and income stacking in peak-bracket years. The interaction between these three factors means that a Roth conversion that appears optimal in isolation can be far less attractive when modeled across its full tax impact. The exact surcharge thresholds adjust annually for inflation, so the specific tiers are not worth memorizing — but the principle is worth internalizing: any significant income event in retirement has a two-year Medicare premium tail.

For couples approaching or in retirement, the practical response is to request that your financial adviser and CPA jointly model multi-year income projections that explicitly account for IRMAA tiers. The goal is not to avoid income — it is to time income strategically. A Roth conversion of $80,000 in a year where other income is low may stay below the first IRMAA threshold. The same conversion in a year with a business distribution or a large capital gain may push into a much more expensive tier. The difference is measurable and plannable — but only if both professionals are looking at the same picture.
