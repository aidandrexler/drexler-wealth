There are financial risks that people plan for eagerly — market crashes, early death, litigation exposure. And there are risks that people avoid planning for because the subject is uncomfortable. Long-term care falls squarely in the second category. Most people would rather discuss their estate plan, their retirement projections, or their asset protection strategy than confront the possibility that they may spend their final years unable to perform the basic activities of daily life without assistance.

This avoidance is expensive. Pfau's Retirement Planning Guidebook cites research showing that more than half of Americans over 65 will require some form of long-term care — extended assistance with bathing, dressing, eating, mobility, or cognitive function. The duration and intensity vary widely. Some people need a few months of home health aide services following a surgery. Others spend years in memory care facilities. The financial cost of the latter is substantial: a private room in a skilled nursing facility in South Florida currently costs between $100,000 and $150,000 per year. Memory care facilities run higher. Home health aides providing full-time care cost in a similar range. These costs are not covered by Medicare beyond short-term skilled nursing following a hospitalization. They are not covered by most health insurance policies. They must be paid from personal assets or from long-term care insurance.

The planning options break into three: self-insuring (maintaining sufficient liquid assets to cover potential LTC costs without insurance), traditional long-term care insurance (a standalone policy that pays a daily or monthly benefit when the insured meets the benefit triggers), and hybrid life/LTC policies (permanent life insurance with an accelerated benefit rider that allows the death benefit to be drawn down tax-free for qualifying long-term care expenses). Each approach has trade-offs. Self-insuring works for families with very significant liquid assets who can absorb a multi-year care event without jeopardizing the surviving spouse's financial security. Traditional LTC policies provide the highest benefit-to-premium ratio but carry premium increase risk and use-it-or-lose-it dynamics. Hybrid policies guarantee a benefit — either a death benefit or an LTC benefit, or some of each — and have become increasingly popular for families who are uncomfortable paying premiums for coverage they may never use.

The planning conversation about LTC is most valuable at ages 55-65 — before insurability becomes questionable and before premium costs become prohibitive. After 65, qualifying for traditional LTC insurance becomes significantly harder, and hybrid policy premiums increase substantially. The conversation is uncomfortable. The consequence of not having it is more uncomfortable.
