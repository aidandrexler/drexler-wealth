For a solo physician, attorney, or consultant with a profitable practice and limited time before retirement, the standard retirement plan options often feel inadequate. A Solo 401(k) allows contributions of approximately $70,000 per year (including employee and employer contributions) for those under 50, and slightly more for those over 50. This is meaningful tax deferral — but for a 52-year-old physician earning $600,000 per year, it barely moves the needle on the tax bill.

The solution that is frequently underused is the cash balance plan — a type of defined benefit plan that can allow contributions of $100,000 to $300,000 or more per year, fully tax-deductible to the business, depending on the owner's age and the actuarial assumptions. A cash balance plan creates a notional account for each participant with a stated benefit growing at a guaranteed crediting rate. The plan is funded by employer contributions calculated by an actuary to meet the stated benefit. For older, higher-earning business owners — particularly those in their 50s who have not accumulated sufficient retirement savings and have limited years to do so — the catch-up capability of a cash balance plan is unmatched by any other retirement savings vehicle.

The tax math is direct. An S-corporation owner contributing $200,000 per year to a cash balance plan reduces taxable business income by $200,000. At a combined federal and state marginal rate of 37%, that is a tax savings of $74,000 per year. Over five years approaching retirement, the cumulative tax savings and deferred growth can meaningfully accelerate the accumulation of retirement assets. The money goes into the plan pre-tax, grows at the guaranteed crediting rate, and is taxed as ordinary income only when distributed in retirement — ideally in years when income and marginal rates are lower.

Cash balance plans are not appropriate for every business. They require consistent profitability to fund the annual actuarial obligation. Businesses with employees must include them in the plan on terms that meet non-discrimination requirements, which can increase costs. And the plan must be established before the end of the tax year for which the deduction is claimed. These constraints are real — but for the right business owner, the combination of a cash balance plan with a 401(k) profit-sharing plan creates a retirement savings capacity that few other structures can match.
