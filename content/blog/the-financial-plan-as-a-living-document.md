title: A Financial Plan That Sits in a Drawer Is Not a Financial Plan
date: January 14, 2026
description: A financial plan produced once and never updated is a historical document, not a planning tool. The value of financial planning is not the document — it is the ongoing process of aligning your resources with your evolving goals.
readTime: 4 min read
---

There is a version of financial planning that most people are familiar with: a comprehensive plan is produced, presented, and filed. The client leaves the meeting with a binder or a PDF. The plan models retirement to age 90, projects portfolio growth under multiple scenarios, identifies insurance gaps, and summarizes estate planning recommendations. It is thorough, impressive, and expensive to produce. It is also immediately out of date.

Hallman and Rosenbloom, in Private Wealth Management (9th ed.), describe wealth management as fundamentally a process — not a product. The value is not in the document delivered at any single point. It is in the ongoing cycle of goal-setting, data gathering, analysis, recommendation, implementation, and review. The plan is the documentation of that process at a moment in time. Life changes constantly: income changes, family circumstances change, tax laws change, goals evolve. A plan that reflects your situation as of two years ago may be actively misleading today.

Evensky and his co-authors describe the investment policy statement — the governing document of a portfolio — as something that should be reviewed and updated whenever the client's circumstances materially change, and confirmed at minimum annually. The same principle applies to the broader financial plan. A life change should trigger a plan review: marriage, divorce, the birth of a child, a significant inheritance, a business sale, a major illness, a job change. Each of these events changes the inputs to the plan. Some change the conclusions dramatically.

The practical test of whether you have a financial plan or a financial planning document is whether it was ever updated after it was produced. If the answer is no, you have a document. A plan is something you return to. Something that shapes decisions throughout the year. Something your adviser references when you call with a question, not something they reconstruct from scratch each time you meet. The most valuable financial planning relationships are the ones where the plan is always current — because the process of keeping it current is ongoing and the adviser is initiating it, not waiting to be asked.
