In every culture that has produced a class of wealthy families, the same proverb appears: shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations. The Japanese say rice paddies to rice paddies. The Chinese say wealth does not survive three generations. The English say clogs to clogs. In every version, the message is the same: wealth created in one generation is spent by the third. James E. Hughes Jr., who spent decades advising some of the world's wealthiest families, asked a different question: why do some families beat this proverb? What do the families that flourish over generations actually do differently?

His answer, developed in *Complete Family Wealth* (Wiley, 2022, with Susan Massenzio and Keith Whitaker), is that the families who endure focus on all five forms of capital — not just financial capital. The five are: human capital (the knowledge, skills, and health of individual family members), intellectual capital (the family's shared wisdom and learning), social capital (the relationships and reputation the family holds in its community), spiritual capital (the family's sense of shared values and purpose), and financial capital (the money). Hughes argues that financial capital is the most brittle of the five — the easiest to lose and the hardest to rebuild once gone. Human capital — a family member's ability to work, create, and contribute — is the most fundamental. Families who invest heavily in developing their human capital while neglecting their financial architecture tend to rebuild wealth. Families who protect their financial capital while allowing human and social capital to atrophy tend to lose everything by the third generation regardless.

The implication for planning is direct. A trust document that perfectly protects financial capital while leaving heirs unprepared to manage, inherit, or steward that capital is not a complete plan. A family that has never had a conversation about its values, its history, or its shared purpose has no foundation for the financial architecture above it. Hughes describes the Big Reveal — the meeting where an adult child is sat down with a lawyer and shown a spreadsheet of the family's wealth for the first time — as one of the most reliably damaging things a family can do. It substitutes a legal briefing for a relationship, and produces heirs who feel burdened by wealth rather than energized by it.

The most sophisticated wealth planning considers all five capitals simultaneously. A financial plan that models your portfolio to age ninety but has never explored what you want your wealth to accomplish — what values you want to transmit, what preparation your heirs have received, what family conversations still need to happen — is technically complete and humanly incomplete. The question worth asking your advisory team is not just whether your estate plan is current, but whether the people who will inherit this estate are prepared to steward it.
