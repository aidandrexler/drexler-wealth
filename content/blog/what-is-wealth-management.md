readTime: 4 min read

The terms wealth management and investment management are used interchangeably in the financial services industry. They are not the same thing. Understanding the difference is one of the most practically valuable things an affluent family can do before selecting a financial adviser.

Investment management is the selection and ongoing supervision of the specific securities in a portfolio. The investment manager has a philosophy and a strategy — perhaps a disciplined value approach to equity selection, or a factor-based passive implementation — and applies it to the assets under management. The goal is typically to maximize risk-adjusted return. Evensky and his co-authors make this distinction explicit in The New Wealth Management (CFA Institute, 2011): a money manager may not necessarily know if a client is male or female, single or married, a doctor, lawyer, or candlestick maker. The money manager's focus is the asset class and the investment mandate. The client's identity is largely irrelevant to that mandate.

Wealth management is something completely different. It is the comprehensive coordination of a client's entire financial life — investment management, financial planning, tax planning, insurance planning, estate planning coordination, retirement planning, business owner planning, and legacy planning — in service of the client's specific goals, circumstances, and values. The wealth manager must know everything about the client: their family, their fears, their goals, their health, their business interests, their existing professional relationships. The wealth manager does not try to maximize returns. They try to help the client achieve their goals — which is a much more specific and much more personal objective. Hallman and Rosenbloom define the core mission of private wealth management as the development and implementation of comprehensive plans for achieving a person's overall financial and personal objectives over their entire economic life cycle.

Most families who think they have a wealth management relationship have an investment management relationship with some planning services added. The test is simple: does your adviser know your will's current provisions? Do they know what your life insurance policy says and who the beneficiaries are? Did they coordinate with your CPA on last year's tax return before it was filed? Have they ever met your estate attorney? If the answer to most of these is no, you have an investment management relationship. That may be exactly what you want. But it is useful to know the difference.
