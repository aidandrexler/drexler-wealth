When wealth advisers talk about family meetings, they usually mean one of two things: a gathering where the adviser presents investment performance to family members, or a multi-day retreat that sounds vaguely therapeutic and costs a significant amount of money to organize. Neither is what James Hughes and his co-authors mean by the term in Complete Family Wealth (Wiley, 2022).

Hughes defines a true family meeting as one where the family owns the agenda — not the adviser. It is not a financial briefing dressed up with family members present. It is a deliberate gathering where family members discuss what matters to them, share news, surface concerns, make decisions together, and build the relationships that allow shared wealth and shared values to endure. The difference between a presentation to family members and a meeting by and for a family is the difference between passive recipients of information and active participants in the family's shared story.

Why do family meetings matter for wealth? Because the primary threat to multigenerational wealth is not market performance or tax law changes or litigation. It is the erosion of trust, shared purpose, and communication among family members as wealth grows and generations multiply. Families that never discuss money, values, or expectations rarely transmit either successfully. Families that discuss these things regularly — imperfectly, sometimes uncomfortably — build the shared understanding that allows wealth to be a source of freedom rather than a source of conflict.

Hughes identifies six questions that a well-designed family meeting should address over time: Why are we doing this together — what is the shared purpose that makes family more than a biological accident? What are our core values and how do we keep them alive? How do we make decisions as a family — who has authority over what? How do we learn together, and how do we prepare rising generation members for the responsibilities they will inherit? How do we handle conflict when it arises? And how do we maintain connection as the family grows larger and more dispersed?

The format can be as simple as an annual dinner where these conversations happen naturally, or as structured as a multi-day retreat with a professional facilitator. What matters is not the format. It is the intention — and the follow-through.
