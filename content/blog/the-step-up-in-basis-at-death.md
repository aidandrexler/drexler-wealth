One of the most powerful and most misunderstood benefits in the federal tax code is the step-up in income tax basis at death. Under IRC §1014, when a person dies, most assets they own receive a new tax basis equal to their fair market value on the date of death. An heir who inherits 1,000 shares of stock worth $500,000 can sell those shares the next day and owe no capital gains tax — regardless of what the original owner paid for them decades earlier. The embedded gain, which could have been taxable for decades if the owner had sold the stock during their lifetime, is permanently eliminated at death.

This benefit is significant for families with appreciated assets: real estate purchased long ago at a fraction of its current value, a family business interest that has grown substantially, a stock portfolio accumulated over a working career. Proper estate planning preserves this benefit. Improper planning can eliminate it.

The step-up applies to assets in the decedent's gross estate for estate tax purposes. It does NOT apply to assets that pass outside the estate — specifically, traditional retirement accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, pensions). Every dollar in a traditional retirement account has never been income-taxed. It will all be taxed as ordinary income when distributed, whether during the owner's lifetime or by the beneficiaries who inherit it. There is no step-up for retirement accounts. This is one of the fundamental reasons that the distinction between taxable accounts (which receive a step-up) and tax-deferred accounts (which do not) is so important in estate planning — and why coordinating asset location decisions with estate planning objectives is a task that requires both a financial adviser and an estate attorney working together.

A specific planning trap: gifting highly appreciated assets during your lifetime to avoid estate taxes. If you give a stock with a cost basis of $10,000 and a current value of $500,000 to your child during your lifetime, the child inherits your original $10,000 basis — and will owe capital gains tax on $490,000 of gain when they sell. If you had held the stock until death, the step-up to $500,000 fair market value would have eliminated that tax permanently. For assets with large embedded gains, dying with them is often more tax-efficient than giving them away — and the decision about which assets to give during life versus hold until death is one of the most consequential estate planning decisions a high-income family makes.
