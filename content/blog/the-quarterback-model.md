Richard Rojeck opens his book on wealth management for high-net-worth individuals with an observation that captures a universal problem: "Almost without exception they have retained the services of capable advisors: attorneys, CPAs, investment bankers, trust officers, investment advisors, and insurance experts. And they have assembled many of the components of a financial plan: wills, trusts, business agreements, investment portfolios, and insurance. But these disparate components were implemented at different times, with different advisors, with differing perspectives, are often uncoordinated and often out of date."

This is not an indictment of the individual advisers. Each is doing their job competently within their domain. The estate attorney drafted an excellent trust. The CPA filed an accurate return. The investment adviser built a well-diversified portfolio. The problem is that no one is responsible for ensuring these pieces form a coherent whole — that the trust is funded, that the beneficiary designations align with the estate plan, that the Roth conversion strategy was modeled before the CPA filed the return, that the buy-sell agreement was reviewed after Connelly. No one is accountable for the space between the advisers.

Hallman and Rosenbloom describe wealth management as the development and implementation of comprehensive plans for achieving a person's overall financial and personal objectives — not a single plan for a single domain, but an integrated strategy across every dimension of a family's financial life. Evensky describes the wealth manager as someone who "must know all of this, as well as the client's dreams, goals, and fears" — contrasting this with the money manager who may not know the client's name. The distinction is the same: comprehensive, coordinated, personal versus focused, standardized, institutional.

Rojeck calls the comprehensive financial planner the quarterback of the advisory team. The quarterback does not throw every pass, block every defender, or execute every play. The quarterback reads the field, calls the plays, and ensures that every specialist on the team is working toward the same objective. In wealth planning, that objective is the client's goals — not any individual adviser's domain expertise.

The families who are best served are those who have identified someone responsible for the integrated picture. Not the adviser who manages the most assets, or the attorney who drafted the most documents, but the person who knows what everyone else is doing and is accountable for making sure it all fits together. That is the service this practice was built to provide.
