Life insurance is frequently described as estate tax-free — meaning the death benefit is generally income tax-free to the beneficiary. This is true. What is frequently overlooked is that life insurance owned by the insured is included in the insured's gross estate for federal estate tax purposes under IRC §2042. For a family approaching estate tax thresholds, a $3M life insurance policy owned personally adds $3M to the taxable estate — potentially generating $1.2M in estate tax on the policy proceeds alone, paid from the very funds that were intended to provide for the family.

The Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust — the ILIT — is the solution. By placing the life insurance policy in an irrevocable trust rather than owning it personally, the death benefit passes to the trust beneficiaries outside the insured's taxable estate. The trust owns the policy. The trust receives the death benefit. The insured holds no incidents of ownership in the policy. Under IRC §2042, if the insured holds no incidents of ownership and the policy is not payable to the estate, the death benefit is not in the taxable estate.

The compliance requirements are the element most often overlooked. Each year, when the insured transfers money to the ILIT to pay the premium, that transfer is a gift to the trust. For the gift to qualify for the annual gift tax exclusion (currently $18,000 per beneficiary per year under IRC §2503(b)), the trust beneficiaries must be given a temporary right to withdraw the gift before the premium is paid. This right must be communicated to each beneficiary in writing — within a specific window, typically 30 days. These notices are called Crummey notices, after the Tax Court case that established the right. Missing them in any given year can reclassify that year's premium payment from an annual exclusion gift to a taxable gift that uses lifetime exemption. Over 15 years of premiums, uncorrected Crummey failures can consume hundreds of thousands of dollars of lifetime exemption that the family did not intend to use.

The practical question to ask your attorney annually: were Crummey notices sent within the required window for every ILIT premium payment this year? The answer should come with documentation. A trust that has been in place for years without this confirmation should prompt an immediate review of the administration history.
